> “That is one of the great mistakes people make: assuming that someone who does menial work does not like thinking. Physical labor is great for the mind, as it leaves all kinds of time to consider the world. Other work, like accounting or scribing, demands little of the body—but siphons energy from the mind.  
>   
> If you wish to become a storyteller, here is a hint: sell your labor, but not your mind. Give me ten hours a day scrubbing a deck, and oh the stories I could imagine. Give me ten hours adding sums, and all you’ll have me imagining at the end is a warm bed and a thought-free evening.”
> 
> “You might think this an unfair moral problem to force upon a simple window washer, but there’s a certain arrogance in that kind of reasoning. A window washer can think, same as anyone else, and their lives are no less complex. And as I’ve warned you, “simple” labor often leaves plenty of time for thought.  
>   
> Yes, intellectuals and scholars are paid to think deep thoughts—but those thoughts are often owned by others. It is a great irony that society tends to look down on those who sell their bodies, but not on those who lease out their minds.”

This challenged greatly the way I think about people who do physical labour. Unfortunately, I thought "they" (even though I also do physical work at my both jobs) were not open to being creative, did not think about things and just followed what the media told them to. In my home mental labour has been always placed on a pedestal of superiority, together with creativity and rhetorics. Physical labour was only perceived as useful as a way to move your body and stay healthy, there was never a deeper connection with the body made or respect for it. My grandma always said that whenever she feels bad, she needs to go out and do something - yet she never meant necessarily talking to others or doing intellectual work. She meant doing chores like cleaning, walking, grocery shopping, and so on. Chores however keep the mind occupied, unlike long, mundane, menial or physical labour, like cleaning, fixing things, trimming and filling, unpacking pallets, driving, filling Excel spreadsheets, mundane and repetitive design work, organising materials. In moments when I do work like this I find often that my mind only then finds the space to come up with new ideas (apart from when I really need to sleep and it's way past my bedtime). But then, I rarely have the space to write these down and they seem to slip away. This quote made me revalue my opinion towards the menial work I and others do. It can leave your mind wondering and go to places out of boredom and space, that when you want it to it seems to never travel to.
