### Episode 1: Learning and getting better at the craft of writing.

> "If the writer gives a character a reason to weep and they weep, the reader won't have to. 
> But if the writer gives the character a reason to weep and they hold it in, the reader will weep."
> 
> "A character's tears let emotions escape the page and the writer's job is to keep the emotions bottled up right in front of the reader, so that the reader will feel them. New writers love tears, but most of the time they are a mistake. This technique seems counter-intuitive but it works remarkably well."

I don't need to add tears to my visual novel - or the player character's reactions to things. It should be the player's choice and the choices should be emotionally limited, so that the reader can feel the emotions. 

- Don't make the characters cry—but the reader.
### Episode 2: Getting ready to start writing a novel.

> "A detailed plan is too easy to follow behind and then discard. 
> But if you are thinking of a plan, try something like this: 
> 
> 1. Initial plotting
> 2. Research and further plotting
> 3. Drafting an outline
> 4. Writing the novel (a page a day)
> 5. Editing the completed work (one chapter a day)."

To this, add deadlines and fixed dates when each of the steps needs to be finished, so that you have something to look forward to and fall back on.

- Don't make a detailed planning because it's easy to fail and discard.
- Write consistently.

> "Start each project with an outline. 
> 
> Have you ever been lost in a forest? I have been for 30 seconds. The fear was palpable and it invaded me when I realised I had no idea in which direction to travel. I remember it with a painful clarity, even though it happened 25 years ago. 
> 
> And this fright is not unlike having written 5 chapters of a novel and then realising you are lost. What happens in the plot now? Should you introduce this character? Does the motorcycle run him over now or later? All of the chapters you've written now seem worthless and it is a disheartening suspicion.
> 
> A chapter-by-chapter summary written before you begin the novel helps avoid this turmoil. This summary or outline doesn't need to be intricate or long. I use one page of summary per scene. And in that summary I figure out which characters will participate in each scene, what the setting is, what the dramatic events are and what additional research I may need. For each scene, it's just a few lines. I prefer making the outline not too long, because most time should be spent on the manuscript."

I also want to spend as little time as possible on my outline, but that's because of the time constraints that I have. In other circumstance I would like to spend a long time on my outline, like one of the leading thriller writers, Jeffrey Deaver. He uses a 150 pages outline:

> "I am a strong advocate of creating a lengthy outline of a novel before writing a single word of a prose. My thrillers take place in a very short time period, a couple days at most, have multiple interesting subplots and characters and have at least 2 surprise endings, often 3. These elements require me to plan ahead. I spend about 8 months outlining and researching. The final outline is around 150 pages long (single spaced, although with large margins to leave space for jotting down references to the research material relevant to the plot). By the end of the outlining process I know exactly what happens in each chapter - when all of the characters enter and leave and where the clues are seated in the story to make those endings work properly, so the reader doesn't feel cheated." - Jeffrey Deaver

My story will also have multiple surprise endings / reveals and I would like to leave clues to them beforehand for the reader to be able to spot and ponder on. I should also think about chapters - maybe they should be days to make it easier for the reader (like in SeekL)?

> "I use (and a lot of writers use) a character list. Whenever I introduce a character, I write their name in the list - it's a Word file. If it's a character I'll use later, I'll copy their description - cut and paste from the novel. This allows me to avoid using the same name or descriptors for two  characters. Orson Scott Card tells of the benefits of the character list: 'Besides names, you'll make a lot of other decisions about the characters - some you will make even before writing the novel, a lot about the character's past, things they are going to do in the story and so on. But you will make many other decisions as you go along, but it will help you a great deal if you jot down from time to time these decisions.'"

I think I will also use this tool in my Obsidian Vault, to keep track of my ideas for characters and then the characters themselves. 

> "I also keep a phrases list. These are phrases or words that I've used, so I don't use them again. Describing a river rapid as a 'foaming tumult' is only good once. A snappy phrase sticks in the writer's mind and it's easy to use it twice, having forgotten the first time you used it. To the reader, the same snappy phrase, although brilliant the first time, is lazy the second time. 
> 
> The phrases list looks like this: 
> 'Chapter 3: 'Gleaming like evil', 'Low brows', 'Placatory smile'."

Good tip! I would never think of doing that! 

> "Another document I use is a post-outline. I add it at the end of every chapter. When you are editing, you will need to know where each scene is, in which chapter - and the pre-writing outline won't work because you will undoubtedly change your story as you write."

I'm not sure if I will do that, since I won't be writing a novel, but a short visual story. 

> "Another document I use is a calendar note. I jot down there any mention of time. Like: 'The documents need to be delivered before Friday' or 'They got married 2 years ago'. This way I can find out easily what happened when."

Again, not sure if I will do it. But I will create it and see if I use it.

> "Final focument I use that is really important is a tie-up-later list. I write down every instance that has not been resolved. If in Chapter 3, we see a security guard inexplicably passing out at his desk, I'll add that to the tie-up later list. I write down 'Explain why the security guard passed out in chapter 3'. I might remember it but I might not. This list prevents unresolved plot points and foreshadowing that leads nowhere.
> 
> A novel is a circle. All questions that have been raised, even the little ones, during the plot, need to be resolved by the end and a tie-up-later list will help do that."

I will try to use that, seems useful and could make keeping up with the story easier for myself as well. 

> "Modern novels average from 70k to 100k words. Any shorter and it's a novella. Any longer and it can't be lifted. Aiming for a certain word length is very helpful, because a target length offers a structure. If you have written 20k words of a suspense novel, you have probably entered the middle-area and usually a lot of things happen during that tie in suspense novels. It is time to start heating things up for the finale."

I want to set up a word length goal for myself - I need to research how many words VN's are usually. I think around 20k maximum for most shorter ones. 

### Episode 3: Go big or go home

> "Readers do not want true life. 
> 
> Novelist and writing instructor Saul Stein says:
> 'The reader of fiction may welcome insight and information - yes, but is primarily interested in an experience different from and greater than his or her everyday experience in life."

This is important information for me, since I will be starting and basing my VN in an office / chat / document-focused (overall, boring) environment. So, I will need to ramp up action and conflict when it does happen - like the terrorist attack in Paper's Please or the non-ethical yet exciting extortions in SeekL. I want my main character (and thus, the reader) to experience a Biblo Baggins like arch - a normal person with a potential to do great things. I need to make those great things even greater, the stakes even higher, the consequences even more dire. 

> "A novel is an amplification of real life. It's wittier, more glamorous, more romantic, more dangerous, it's faster and bigger. There is more going on, there is more at stake."

### Episode 4: Pace, the first sentence, back-story, over-description

> Pace is the rate in which the plot unfolds. 

> Bernard Cornwell in his Richard Sharp's series always begins with a fast pace that will be sustained throughout the book. 

That doesn't mean a lot of action - it can also mean that a lot is going on in the story - emotionally, conversationally, or even on the micro scale. 

> Steven King says you can overdo the speed thing. If the pace is too fast the writer can confuse or wear out the reader. 

This is important for me to remember and to take into account. My reveals need to combine fast pacing and carefully and entertainingly (?) explained details so that the reader knows what's going on and feels the joy of the mystery being solved.

> Albert Zuckerman, the novelist and writing instructor says: "The one aspect of a blockbuster novel structure that usually keeps the reader turning the pages more than any other is pace. Storytelling that moves relentlessly forward, constantly respositioning the characters and posing ever new dramatic questions in the reader's mind."

That's true. I never noticed that consciously, but when reading novels I mainly keep on reading to get my questions answered, like: what will happen to Elantris and Radeon, how will Harry defeat Voldemort, who hides behind the mysterious A.B.C. acronym? I need to add more questions for the readers to keep them interested instead of immediately revealing everything - including such fundamental things as the world-building, character backgrounds and names, and so on.
